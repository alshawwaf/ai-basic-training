Drag the slider. Nothing about the architecture is changing across these stages — only the values of the billions of weights, after billions of "predict, compare, nudge" updates. Reasoning, world knowledge, and coding ability are all emergent side-effects of getting good at one thing: predicting the next token.

One step of pretraining, in numbers

Suppose the model sees the input "The firewall blocked the" and outputs the following probability distribution over the next token:

Candidate tokenModel probabilityBar
door0.40
connection0.20
traffic0.05
malicious ← correct0.03
Cross-entropy loss for this example:
loss = -log(P[correct]) = -log(0.03) ≈ 3.51

That single number drives backpropagation: the gradient of the loss with respect to every weight is computed, and each weight is nudged a tiny step in the direction that would have made malicious slightly more likely (and door slightly less likely) the next time. After one update, this change is invisible. After a trillion updates, the weights have absorbed the entire structure of human language.

The full pipeline: pretraining vs fine-tuning vs inference

Three phases that are often confused. Pretraining is by far the most expensive — and the only one you will never do yourself.

PhaseWhoCostWhat it produces
Pretraining Big labs only
(OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, Meta)		 $50M–$200M
2–6 months on 10k+ H100s		 A base model that knows language but is not yet a chatbot
Fine-tuning Same labs, or you on a small open-weight model $1k–$10M
per dataset		 A model specialised for a domain or aligned to instructions
Inference You, every API call fractions of a cent per query The actual answer to your prompt
What this means for you: when you call an LLM API, you are renting the output of a $100M training run. You cannot change the weights — the only levers you have are the tokens you put in front of the model. Prompting, system prompts, and retrieval (RAG) are the only tools you actually control. Every later lesson in this stage is downstream of that fact.

Cybersecurity tie-in: A pretrained model's knowledge has a hard cutoff at the date training ended. It does not know about a CVE published yesterday, the patch your team shipped this morning, or the policy your CISO updated last week. RAG (Lesson 4.4) exists specifically to inject fresh threat intel and internal docs at inference time, without retraining anything — because retraining costs nine figures.
