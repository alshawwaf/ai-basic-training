Words become fingerprints

A computer can't read text. So each word gets replaced by 4 numbers — its embedding "fingerprint". Each number tracks one property of meaning. Words with similar meanings end up with similar fingerprints, and the model learns this from billions of examples.

Real LLMs use 768 to 4 096 numbers per word. We use 4 here so you can read every one of them.

What the 4 numbers mean

In a real LLM the dimensions are abstract — humans can't name them. In this toy model we hand-picked four meanings so you can read each fingerprint at a glance.

Threat
High → danger (malicious, attack)
Low → safe (benign, normal)
Network
High → networking term (network, port, traffic)
Action
High → doing/permitting (scan, allowed)
Negative → stopping (blocked, malicious)
Detection
High → spotting/alerting (alert, detected)

Reading a fingerprint

Here is the embedding vector for the word "attack" — four numbers that encode its meaning:

DimensionValueInterpretation
Threat+0.95Very high — makes sense
Network+0.40Moderately network-related
Action-0.30Negative — harmful / destructive
Detection+0.50Something you'd want to detect

Each value makes intuitive sense. The model never saw a label saying "attack is threatening" — it learned this from how the word appears in context across billions of sentences. In the next step, you'll compare two fingerprints side-by-side and see how the model tells synonyms from opposites.

Cybersecurity tie-in: In real LLMs, "malware" and "ransomware" end up with similar fingerprints because they appear in similar contexts in the training data. This is how the model "knows" they are related — not from a dictionary, but from distributional semantics: words that hang out with the same neighbours mean similar things.
